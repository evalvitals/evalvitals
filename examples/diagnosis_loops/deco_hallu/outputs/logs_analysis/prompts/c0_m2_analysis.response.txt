CONCLUSION: The failures are genuine language-prior–driven false detections (object hallucination) by qwen3-vl-2b-instruct, not an artifact of the scoring or prompt template: the model says "Yes" to absent objects because it relies on scene-plausibility priors rather than verifying visual evidence, and the attention analysis shows that on failure cases the model's image attention is misallocated—peaking on background/contextually-plausible patches rather than on evidence of the queried object. Critically, prompt-level mitigations (describe-first, sensitivity framing) do not fix it, so the defect is in the model's grounded-verification behavior, not the question phrasing.

EVIDENCE_CHAIN:
- The `generated:probe1.false_detection` / `explored.probe1_positive` signal jumped out first: effect = +0.946 (CI 0.875–1.000) means an independent yes/no probe reproduces the "Yes" almost perfectly on exactly the failure set, showing the false detection is a stable, reproducible model behavior rather than noise.
- This maps directly onto the protocol's stated failure pattern—absent-object questions answered "Yes"—and confirms these are true false positives scored against per-question ground truth, not mislabeled present-object controls.
- Corroboration comes from `relative_attention` (max/mean weight and focus_share all REJECT H0, Kendall τ ≈ +0.25–0.32, fail-mean max weight 196.6 vs pass 91.1), indicating failures carry abnormal attention concentration; the only tension is that `explored.low_focus_share` is also elevated on failures (+0.59), suggesting attention is bimodal—either spiking on a wrong patch or diffusely smeared—rather than cleanly focused on the (nonexistent) object.

QUALITATIVE:
- In the fail exemplar (rainy umbrella scene) attention spreads over the grass/ground background, while in the pass exemplar (airport) it locks tightly onto the actual backpack—visually, hallucinations coincide with attention landing on plausible-but-empty context regions.
- The surprising part is that the prompt-contrast interventions are inconclusive and even slightly net-negative (describe_first broke 3 / fixed 1), so encouraging the model to "look first" does not curb the priors—the false-positive bias is baked into the model rather than fixable by instruction.
